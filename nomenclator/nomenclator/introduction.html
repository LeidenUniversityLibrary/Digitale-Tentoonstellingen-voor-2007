Bibliotheken Tentoonstellingen Nomenclator

Introduction

R. Breugelmans

Leiden University opened its new library on 24 May 1595.[1]  It was no doubt both a stately and a festive event, held in the new upper floor built to house the library in the former Beguine church on the Rapenburg. It was graced by the attendance of the governors of the university - namely the three Curators appointed by the States of Holland, and the four Burgomasters of Leiden - as well as the professors and other invited guests, among them the Leiden town clerk Jan van Hout and the acting librarian Petrus Bertius.[2] It was these two men who had actually carried out the work: Van Hout was deeply involved in the building and furnishing, while Bertius had taken charge of the more technical-librarianship side, such as the classification of the books (and so, their division among the shelves), though even here Van Hout had given advice.

A highlight of the day, along with the handing over of the library keys to those who had a right to them, was the appearance of the printed catalogue of the library. This Nomenclator autorum omnium, quorum libri vel manuscripti, vel typis expressi exstant in Bibliotheca Academiae Lugduno-Batavae (List of all authors whose books, whether manuscript or printed, are available in Leiden University Library) presents a number of interesting aspects. It is not only the earliest complete printed catalogue of any institutional library in the world, it also shows clearly which books were available for study at a time when most scholars had no recourse to extensive working-libraries of their own [3] and, just as today, could not generally buy the multi-volume (and therefore expensive) standard works and text editions. This latter aspect also makes the catalogue a 'source for the sources' of the Leiden scholarly work of that day.

It is tempting to try to find an explanation why Leiden University set the trend for a printed catalogue. Bertius in his preface does not elaborate on the subject and lack of further direct evidence makes it necessary to speculate. As the collection of the library was available on open shelving and the books were arranged according to their subject-matter, there was in fact no need for the visitors and readers to find the books with the help of the catalogue.

One obvious reason for the publication of the Nomenclator must have been the wish to present the university to the outside world. Its founding had in itself been an astute political act. In the middle of the war against the Spaniards the rebellious northern provinces of the Netherlands had obtained their first protestant institution of higher education. Famous scholars, such as J.J. Scaliger, had been invited to professorships, and this, again, may be interpreted in terms of public relations management.

It must have been a matter of pride as well to show what had indeed been achieved by the young university, which existed only twenty years and possessed a library only since 1587. Yet another purpose of the Nomenclator may have been to invite donations. Quite some attention is devoted in the Nomenclator to donators of books. This may have had the effect that prospective ones were implicitly adhorted to join the legion of benefactors to the library.

Publishing the catalogue of the university library was yet another move which would enhance Leiden's reputation as a centre of learning. And it must have been quite successful, certainly to judge by the numerous students from protestant regions who flocked to Leiden. That in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries throughout Europe so many more library catalogues were published once more shows that publishing the Nomenclator was a stroke of genius. Would it be too far fetched to see Jan van Hout's organizational skills and managerial talents also behind this enterprise?

The catalogue is dedicated to the governors of the university. In his foreword addressed to them (folios A2r to B2v), Bertius regrets the loss of so many writings from Antiquity, and complains that what does survive from that time, and even contemporary writings, are so little studied. He therefore finds it especially commendable that the governors would wish to devote so much care (and money   [4] ) to this library. Bertius says that he had to retrieve the books from scattered places, and that he had the bindings and title-pages ('involucris sittybisque') stamped with the figure of Minerva and the words ACAD LVGD. He also had the names of the authors and the titles added in manuscript on the backs of the books. He further reports that each field of study was assigned its own kind of alphabet. The 'plutei' (open bookcases) in which the books were placed were also designated with letters of these alphabets: Hebrew for theology (see folios B3r to B4v and gatherings C, D, 2C, and 3C in the catalogue), roman capitals for jurisprudence (gathering E), roman lowercase for medicine (gathering F), italic lowercase for history (gathering G), Greek lowercase for philosophy (gathering H), civilité capitals for mathematics (gathering I) and lowercase black letter for literature (gathering K). Within each pluteus, the books were numbered sequentially. Where additional plutei were later needed for any field of study, they were given the successive letters of the relevant alphabet. This division applies only to the folio volumes, which were attached to the plutei with brass chains - not iron because of the danger of rust ('non ferreis quidem, propter rubiginem'). The smaller formats (gathering L) were arranged according to field of study, but without further numbering, in two closed bookcases along the walls. Finally, Bertius reports that he has set up a special bookcase for the publications of those who are in any way connected with the university (gathering M).[5]

The Nomenclator was printed in an edition of 625 copies by Franciscus Raphelengius, who charged 62 guilders and 15 stuivers on 17 June 1595.[6] It survives in two issues: the first comprises only gatherings A to M, and that is unquestionably the form in which the catalogue was presented on 24 May 1595. The second [7] includes the additional gatherings 2C and 3C (which could better have been signed as 2D and 3D), and N and 0. These last two contain the names of the donors and their donations received by the library up to 11 August 1595, beginning with the Biblia regia from Willem van Oranje. It is followed by the names of the professor of Greek Bonaventura Vulcanius; the professor of theology Johannes Holmannus, who had died at the end of 1586; the professor of Hebrew and printer/publisher Franciscus Raphelengius, who donated an Aetius manuscript in 1590 and on 1 July 1595 added all books 'written in this Academy and published by him'; Henricus Bredius; the Burgomasters of Leiden, who turned over to the library the books that Christoffel Plantin had originally donated to the city on 11 January 1585; Jan van Hout; the Court of Holland, which donated several books including the famous Talmud extensively described on folios 2Clr to 3Clr; and several others.[8] The collection of works by Leiden professors and others connected with the university was considerably expanded by Raphelengius's second donation and that from the Leiden Burgomasters, which might have resulted in a number of duplicates. The remainder of gathering 3C contains the descriptions of a few more or less theological works and an index to the Monumenta s . patrum orthodoxographa (Basel 1569). Another notable element of this second issue of the Nomenclator is a series of addendum slips pasted on folios E2r, F1v, G4r, H3r, and K1v, which contain descriptions of the most recent donations - only for the folio volumes, of course.

The Nomenclator is arranged as a shelf-list. The books are reported in the order in which they stood in their cases. There are only a few exceptions: if books from different fields of study are bound together, any additional title appears in its own division with a note of the other division where it is to be found in pluteus x at number y. A clear subdivision in the arrangement of the books can be seen only in certain fields. That is the case for example in theology: first come the bibles, then the commentaries in the biblical order, then the Church Fathers and other primary authors, and finally the secondary authors. In philosophy too, commentaries on Aristotle are placed together as a group. The original catalogue had no alphabetical index.

Is it possible to determine the extent of the library from the information in the Nomenclator? Exact counts are possible only for the books listed in gatherings A to L, 2C, and 3C. These were 407 folio volumes [9] comprising 338 titles. These are divided among the fields of study as follows:

field volumes titles
theology 158 103
jurisprudence 47 28
medicine 36 29
history 61 66
philosophy 60 67
mathematics 15 22
literature 30 23

The smaller formats comprise 104 titles, including the one folio volume mentioned earlier; it is no longer possible to determine the number of volumes in which these were bound. Partly due to the lack of comparable figures, it is now extremely difficult to judge how the new library, with its somewhat more than 442 titles, compares in size to others of the period.

We can say something about the arrangement of the library reading room. The well-known 1610 print by Jan Cornelis Woudanus, engraved by Willem Swanenburgh, gives a reasonable if not always trustworthy image. Of course, the number of plutei had by then increased (to 22), and the bookcase with the Legatum Scaligerianum had been added only in 1609. The twelve or thirteen [10] plutei were arranged in two rows. Each pluteus had a protruding sloped shelf where the books could be laid for reading.


The library in 1610

The books stood on their heads in the bookcase with their spines facing out, secured with the brass chains already mentioned,[11] which in turn were secured by a ring to an iron rod.[12] There may have been a bench attached to the back of each pluteus where the reader working at the following pluteus could sit. The two Hondius globes could not have been there yet in 1595, although there were two brass 'sphaerae'. There were three closed bookcases for the smaller formats, one of which was intended for the publications of the members of the Leiden academic community. The walls were no doubt adorned with various prints, maps, etc.

The acquisition of books for the library differed little in essence from that of today. At that time they came exclusively from Leiden booksellers, the most eminent among them being Franciscus Raphelengius and Louis Elsevier, and from private individuals, both by purchase and by donation. There was still no antiquarian purchasing in those days, unless one counts the purchases at auctions, of which we unfortunately know nothing more. In any case, the Nomenclator makes it clear that a representative scholarly library was built up in this way thanks to the efforts of many. Its intellectual value far exceeds its material value, and it gave, gives, and will continue to give many scholars both in and outside of Leiden the opportunity to carry out their work.
